# Long Live the Spirit of Pioneering

The Xinjiang People's Publishing House sent me ten poetry manuscripts at once, requesting that I write a preface for this forthcoming series. I set aside all my other work and spent a full half-month reading them through, word by word, line by line. It was a true privilege to become acquainted with such a large group of Xinjiang poets through their songs—middle-aged and young poets who began writing in the fifties, sixties, and seventies, as well as poets from fraternal ethnic minorities. I could not help but be moved; I had so many thoughts that I initially did not know where to begin.

Last September, I took a trip to Xinjiang. However, because my workplace repeatedly sent telegrams urging me to return home for organizational reform, I was forced to cut my original itinerary short. I made Yining my temporary final stop and turned back to Urumqi. My stay was brief, and my meetings were limited; nevertheless, even so, I am confident that I grasped a bit of Xinjiang's unique "poetic essence."

What is this unique poetic essence of Xinjiang? Closing my eyes in reflection, I believe the word "Pioneering" (Kaituo) can perhaps summarize it all.

I remember on my journey, whenever I asked where the next stop for rest would be, the answer was always the same: "Another X hundred miles." How vast! Xinjiang! This magnificent spirit and expansive realm left a very strong impression on me.

It must be said that most of the poems included in this series generally reflect this characteristic. Whether it be ice peaks, the "Fire Island" (Turpan), grasslands, lakes, deserts, the Gobi, yurts, corrals, reclamation, hunting, vineyards, karez (irrigation systems), poplars, tower pines, mosques, bazaars, buzkashi (sheep dragging), "girl chase" (a traditional folk game), the rawap, or the dombra... these are the waters and soils of this land, its landscapes, its people, and their mindsets.

No one without firsthand experience could possibly write these. This confirms the saying: "Life is the only source of creation." Some might shake their heads: "The same old story!"

Indeed, truths repeatedly tested by practice often sound like clichés. However, if people truly treat them as mere clichés, they are being far too reckless. I can assert that the victim will not be anyone else, but oneself.

Life is inexhaustible; no one has the right to boast: "My life is already enough." It is never enough. Moreover, the poetic inspiration that bursts forth from life has always been a shy, timid angel; she will never appear in your field of vision a second time in exactly the same way. The poet's task is to capture this fleeting spirit in time and fix it with words—or, one might say, to photocopy it with words.

Everything depends on the richness of one's depth in life. I think that among the authors in this series, those who have achieved success are all mindful observers of life.

What is especially precious is that life in Xinjiang is both the life of a pioneer and a life that is constantly being further pioneered. In the relationship between humans and nature, pioneering means initiative and struggle, fearlessness, and placing hope in the future. Therefore, it requires ideals, courage, and perseverance. A Xinjiang poet should naturally possess the temperament of a pioneer. In the relationship between humans and art, pioneering means exploration and creation, innovation, and a refusal to believe that every problem has a ready-made answer. Thus, the realism upheld by a pioneer poet should not merely be a creative method, but an open system of artistic thought. It must demonstrate a comprehensive and transformative breadth of spirit, and it must strictly adhere to the principle of "maintaining my own essence while utilizing what is useful to me."

Looking across the Xinjiang poetry scene in recent years, it is indeed heartening. Several representative poets have written representative works that possess a clear sense of the era and a solid sense of history. They have breadth of mind, power, and a stern, heroic beauty—a proper blend of ruggedness and delicacy. Most importantly, they have a tomorrow.

Looking to the future, I am optimistic about the prospects of Xinjiang's "New Poetry" movement. The greatest basis for this optimism is that Xinjiang has talent—because pioneering produces talent. As long as the spirit of pioneering is not lost, Xinjiang's poetry will surely have an even more brilliant and broader future.

This shall serve as the preface.

**January 11, 1984, in Hefei**
