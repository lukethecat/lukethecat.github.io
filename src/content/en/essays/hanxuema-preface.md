# Preface: Blood-Sweating Horses

**By Zhang Xiaoping**

## 1

As time passes, when I float upon the river of Li Yu’s poetry, I suddenly feel a kind of drifting—in the sense of both life and time—a heat within melancholy, a sadness within sentimentality, a Western-style muteness combined with a Russian-style lightness. During those "unprecedented" periods of history, he wandered across the world in a state of tragic displacement. While that tragic wandering was certainly a gift of the times, Li Yu himself seems to possess a natural temperament for drifting. Due to his unique personal character and specific life background, what he gained from this drifting was not a carefree openness, but rather melancholy and a haunting elegance (youwan). The form of his drifting determined that he could not perform a powerful spiritual explosion within the ranks of poetry; instead, he could only offer a low murmur, like the sound of a bamboo flute, through the melancholy and elegance of his wandering. The sharp songs and military rhythms of history's clashing swords and armored horses, once settled in Li Yu’s spiritual world, are stated in the tones of a vertical flute. This seems to have determined that Li Yu, with the mindset of a drifter, slowly chants along a lonely, long journey.

Drifting achieved Li Yu’s poetry. I am referring to his Western Regions historical series, *Blood-Sweating Horses*. It centrally and completely embodies Li Yu’s spiritual temperament. The most important significance of *Blood-Sweating Horses* is its breakthrough pioneering of Western frontier poetic subjects. Through poetry, he has performed an exquisite extension and expansion of the history, culture, and geography of the Western Regions. This is the Silk Road—once bustling and prosperous, then silent—starting from ancient Chang'an, passing through Dunhuang to the Western Regions, and then crossing Eurasia to ancient Rome. This is the brilliant history buried underground in the vast area known as the Western Regions since the Western Han Dynasty. In a narrow sense, the "Western Regions" refers to the territory west of the Han Dynasty's Yume Pass and east of the Congling (Pamir Plateau) out of the Karakoram Mountains. The Western Regions was the vital passage and distribution center for economic and cultural exchange between the East and the West. Unquestionably, the intellectual and spiritual weight of the "Western Regions" concept belongs deeply to poetry; it once represented a magnificent spiritual image and posture of Han and Tang China. Let us mount our blood-sweating horses and travel through the Western Regions in our minds.

*Blood-Sweating Horses* is a series of historical poems about the Western Regions, its scale approaching that of an epic. Regarding the historical and cultural knowledge it involves, I believe he has basically captured every intensely reflective facet of this regional history, thereby constructing a complete poetic world of Western Regions history. The breadth, number, and completeness of the geographical scope, historical figures, and historical events it involves are unprecedented in previous poetry. I do not know when the poet Li Yu acquired the structural consciousness of an architect, but this massive constructive and pioneering nature demonstrates his intellectual and spiritual capacity, as well as his sense of emotional mission in committing to the Western world. A region like the West, where brilliant historical culture has accumulated, easily triggers our nostalgia for the past; these are historical fossils that can be traced upon the Western land. Those other, vanished parts can only be found through historical records. But history books are merely history books; to represent them as a living poetic world requires the poet to have a network of longitude and latitude in his mind that is even more complete than history itself. Even if Li Yu could hear a faint historical sound at the ruins of some ancient kingdom, it would be difficult to hear anything beneath the entire West and the land of the Western Regions. This structural network appeared gradually beneath his drifting feet; from an initial state of scattered blocks and dots, it was gradually lengthened, unfolded, and connected. This represents the artistic direction of many Western frontier poets. In the poetry of various Western poets, we can see the reflection of that historical mirror—that historical light, in some poets, even becomes an invisible spiritual and emotional motif. But none of them walked directly into history; they initially operated within the spiritual space of realistic existence, moving from a concern for people and society to a concern for people and nature, and for their own lives. Once they reach such a spiritual capacity, they inevitably possess a historical consciousness; they will inevitably acknowledge and reassess historical culture and the ancient, powerful life force of humanity. Otherwise, the internal spiritual drive in their lives will not be replenished, and because it is insufficient, they will fall into the exhaustion of artistic regenerative power.

Li Yu is pursuing a spirit during his drifting. Like the writer Zhang Chengzhi and the poet Chang Yao, Li Yu performed this pursuit through a complete regional historical form. The continuous wars, the Silk Road with bows drawn in front and camel teams behind—these became the longitude and latitude of his Western Regions historical poetry. The living souls arranged among them shimmer within this framework. The tireless killing, the relentless diplomatic marriages, the shared creation of material culture—this is the chaotic original state of history. And war is the lever that pries history, the proclamation of life toward history. "His ghost-like flickering black steed / stands frozen like a sculpture under the Qilian Mountains / yet the iron hooves still strike frequently... the sound of the hooves / breaks the death-like silence / in the steel symphony like a hurricane that has just passed / becoming clearer and more tragic / like a mournful mourning song / soothing the souls that have just fought / and flying into his dreams again." This is about Huo Qubing. This can roughly be seen as the emotional keynote of Li Yu’s view on ancient frontier wars. It is tragic, a deep sentimentality and grief. The sparks and flames of life perishing are splashed during the historical process of weapons clashing. After this process, when a powerful life shows its fragility through death, when a fierce life can no longer make a response in an instant and shows submission to death, what else can we say?

## 2

Follow Li Yu's *Blood-Sweating Horses* on a long, solitary journey of history, culture, and spirit. Li Yu moves slowly through the long night in a lonely, foreign land. In the depths of the vanished, vast history, he reawakens those lonely camel caravans and follows their silhouettes as they extend toward the western and eastern borders: "Zhang Qian smiled / The pioneer of the Silk Road smiled on horseback / Looking west / Toward the direction of the Great Yuezhi / Not yet visible / Crimson blood dripped from his parched lips." From the Central Asian continent, his brushstrokes extend all the way to the "dark blue Persian Gulf," where Gan Ying, the first diplomatic envoy sent to Rome during the Eastern Han Dynasty, was blocked and turned back: "The gorgeous camel team stands quietly on the beach / Gan Ying stands quietly on the beach / The cool tide quietly surges onto his boots / And quietly recedes / O, the Rome of dreams / O, the Rome of longing / O, the Rome of searching / Where is the western end of the long Silk Road / That shimmers with the luster of silk?" Bitter seawater mixed with bitter tears once again fell into the dark blue Persian Gulf.

These two figures were undoubtedly written by Li Yu as images of pioneers in Western Regions history; the missions they undertook were arduous, heavy, and unprecedented. Both the winner and the loser, with the same perseverance and courage in hardship, displayed the immortal spirit of life. Westerners seem to be like this; they were like this thousands of years ago, and they are still like this thousands of years later, writing the breadth of life with a tenacious spirit of reclamation and pioneering. In many Western frontier poems, this pioneering spirit is often embodied in the lives of the victors, manifesting as a majestic and high-pitched form. What we perceive in the images of Zhang Qian and Gan Ying, however, is the state of the soul’s solitude in a moment—the sharp sense of pain, the sense of support when a great life falls to a weak layer, and the spiritual and psychological transformation displayed after that moment. It does not show a successful outcome, but through the instantaneous rebirth of psychological transformation, it predicts the inevitable direction of the life spirit. This setback in the hardship of life—this instantaneous exposure of a great life falling into a weak state—undoubtedly comes from Li Yu’s melancholy and sentimental spiritual world. Yet the emotional power it embodies in art is a decisive refusal of depression. In the low valley of a weak state, life can hesitate and wander, but ultimately it will be a more firm and powerful life advance after the psychological transformation, rather than the disintegration and collapse of a spirit that has lost its support. Toughness makes one majestic, while such sentimentality makes one sharp and generous. What life presents at this level is the kind of concealment of pain with an expressionless, calm breadth that I mentioned earlier—a masculine air with a sense of setback and weather-beaten resilience.

## 3

The beauty of a sentimentally aching life, together with toughness and majesty, displays the essence of the Western person. In the Western Regions zone of the Silk Road, there is also a silk-like richness and brilliance: silk, jade, musical instruments, red felt carpets, post houses, the Sogdian Whirl (Hu Xuan) dance, singing and dancing girls... This is the Central Asian flavor of the peaceful periods on the Silk Road, a cluster of flowers where the material cultures of High Tang China and Persia met. At that time, as many as ten ethnic groups and their tribes, after the storms of war, drank heartily and danced; they stood under the brilliant sunlight, like the unceasing Dionysian feast under the sun rendered by Rubens in his oil painting *The Kermesse*. This determined the passionate and gorgeous life attitude and aspirations of the people in this region. We can still hear that distant historical echo in their hearty singing and dancing under the poplar forests and grape trellises. As a display of complete regional history, Li Yu would not neglect this. "A branch of elegant black wild poppy has withered / its dark brown petals still emit an intoxicating fragrance / her tragic tears / she shed them on the dark brown petals of the wild poppy / her tragic tears / suddenly merged with the dew on the petals..." This is a scene of a hunting trip by Princess Xijun, a Han Dynasty princess from Southern China married far away to the Western Regions as a Wusun queen. The poet then writes of Xijun and the light cavalry of the guard galloping under the hills of the pasture, finally ending abruptly with: "Xijun reined in her horse and stood / the galloping Wusun King and his guard / once again clustered around her." This is undoubtedly a gorgeous beauty. Although the poem is very short, it gives a strong sense of open, expansive movement. The fragrance of the queen in military attire, the light cavalry, and the wildness of the poppy fields make us feel the gorgeous joy of life under a peaceful sky, reminding us of the flower-like cherishing of this Han queen from a foreign land by the frontier peoples. After the intense military confrontation in this region, the various ethnic groups plucked the music and dance of life.

Yet such splendor is still endowed by Li Yu with the melancholy and sentimentality from the depths of his spirit. He wrote about the other part of Xijun's life—the daughterly emotions that returned to this foreign queen in the quiet of the night, the psychological loneliness that arose when she missed her relatives in her hometown, and her looking back at the romantic life of her childhood in her hometown amidst the mournful Pipa music of the foreign land. This is a complex emotional contradiction; she left her homeland for the Western Regions, and the brilliance of her emotions and life also shone on this land. Yet that sense of drifting at the end of the world—that mournful feeling of a lonely goose lost from the flock—still could not be fundamentally dispelled. She was not the strong husbands reclaiming the border, nor even a figure with a subjective sense of mission. Her status as a queen could not cover the daughterly heart deep within her, nor could it cut her lingering attachment to the bloodlines of her homeland, even though part of her life’s emotions also merged into this Wusun Kingdom of the Western Regions. "Xijun fell into a deep sleep / resting here forever / the mournful musical phrases of her 'Song of Longing for Home' / still linger / her soul / suddenly turned into a oriole / flying out of the gorgeous yurt," deeply kissing this vast foreign land and flying straight toward the southeast, her tearful pupils superimposed with the colorful markets of Chengdu. Is this mournful, gorgeous beauty the deep inner meaning of Western frontier poetry? Certainly it is. The life of the Western person is embodied in all abnormal living and emotional environments; it is a resilient and long-lasting life behavior that cannot be possessed in a normal living environment. They invest their lives into it, showing a behavioral move of life breaking through inherent states of existence, forming a transcendence of life. Their strength is the strength of the transcenders; their sentimentality is also the sentimentality of the transcenders’ souls being scorched. This constitutes the complete emotion of their lives, forming the contradiction and fullness of life, and thus possesses more logical demonstration power and the infectious power of artistic emotion. This will be a question we must clarify regarding the life inner meaning of Western frontier poetry. The beauty of the Yang (masculine) is the heat and magnificence of the daily tide on the Western highlands; the beauty of the Yin (feminine) is the clear and infinite moonlight on the Western highlands. What they both embody is a kind of vastness—the vastness after the tense state of tempering and support when life is invested in an abnormal living environment. This is the value and meaning I see from Li Yu’s poetry. It sees and excavates an essence of Western frontier poetry from another perspective and corrects our bias in understanding Western frontier poetry.

I have followed French artists and their Paris, Elytis and his Aegean Sea, but what interests me more are Neruda, Márquez, and Paz for their rough and mysterious South America, and Senghor for Senegal with its dark nights and tam-tam drums. I believe in the corresponding transformation of life and art, and with full estimation, I believe in the powerful internal driving effect of raw regional culture on poetry as an art of life. I believe in its explosive power, and also in its endurance, which will continue to show its strength through several generations.

Oh, in the distant Western Regions, we see those blood-sweating horses galloping and roaring to their heart's content, entering the heights of life. Singers and poets are quietly watching them, with an expression like expecting a sacred scripture.
