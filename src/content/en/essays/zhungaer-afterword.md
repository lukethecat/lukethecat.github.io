# Lyrical Flute Notes Rising from the Desert—Reading *Verses from Junggar*

**By Ah Hong**

## A

Li Yu invited me to write a review for his forthcoming debut collection, *Verses from Junggar*.

I felt a sense of trepidation.

How could I not?

Chernyshevsky said: "The more we know about a poet's character, life, and the people he encounters, the more we can see a portrait of a living person in his works."

I regret that when I met Li Yu at the "Green Wind" poetry gathering last September, I did not have the foresight to talk more with him about his life. That was an opportunity to understand his journey!

Now, separated by vast distances, with clouds flying and eagles circling over ten thousand miles, communication is difficult.

I only know from the imitation-Song typeface in his early published works that he was once a "knowledgeable youth" (Zhiqing).

I only know from a review of New Frontier Poetry that during those "unprecedented" days, he "lived a period of tragic wandering"; I know he was exiled from a new oasis city to a military reclamation farm on the edge of the Gurbantünggüt Desert, and that he entered the poetic scene in 1972 singing the "Song of the First Sickle."

I only know he recently ended more than twenty years of teaching and was transferred to work for a newspaper in Urumqi. The supplement he manages features many famous names and is excellently edited.

Beyond that, there is this volume, *Verses from Junggar*.

As these various pieces of information collide and merge, I have formed the following impression of Li Yu:

He is a builder of the reclamation areas and a pioneer of the oasis through and through. In Junggar, in the Gurbantünggüt, he worked industriously; he shed sweat there, and perhaps even tears!

He has ideals, conviction, and a spirit that will not sink even if dropped into the ocean.

He labored, lived, and wrote poetry. On the battlefield of pioneering the oasis, he was a frontline correspondent—a poet who also wielded a *kantuman* (hoe).

He has many dreams. He knows how to dream. In a material wasteland, bearing a heavy spiritual burden, he struggled and trekked with the spirit of "For love, Baghdad is not too far" to realize his dreams. One should say his dreams are being realized one by one.

Perhaps one could say: Li Yu's path in life has been quite rugged and bumpy. Yet—and please forgive me for saying something potentially unpopular—this rugged path has actually "completed" Li Yu. Think of the poets throughout history; how many were raised on sugar-water? On the contrary, most had extraordinary experiences. Wang Anshi said: "As the year ages, the roots grow stronger; as the sun scorches, the leaves grow brighter." Byron said: "Adversity is the path to truth." Belinsky said: "Misfortune is the best university." Li Yu moved from his southern hometown to Xinjiang to pioneer the oasis, throwing himself into the battle for the development of Xinjiang, which will forever be praised in history books. Of course, life was hard, but wasn't it precisely this hard life that allowed him to deeply familiarize himself with and understand the pioneers? Wasn't it this life that tempered his will and soul? Wasn't it this that allowed him to offer this book, *Verses from Junggar*? Wasn't it this that made him a rising star of poetry?

This book, *Verses from Junggar*,
Is the scroll of his life experience,
The fluorescent screen of his spiritual realm,
And the lyrical flute notes rising from the great desert!

## B

I remember the Russian poet Nekrasov once wrote some poems that the critic Belinsky considered to be mere imitations. At that time, Nekrasov was in the "childhood" stage of his poetic creation.

As I read Li Yu's poems, I found that he too had a childhood stage of creation.

"Song of the First Sickle" was his debut work. Published in 1972, it inevitably bears the marks of that period. Its placement as the first piece in this collection may be for commemorative reasons.

The conception and language of this poem are average, and the intellectual depth is relatively shallow. Indeed, in the early draft of this collection I saw, there were several such poems.

I point this out because I think many poems received by literary journals are exactly like this—healthy in emotion and complete in structure, but lacking that certain something indispensable to the art of poetry.

I believe that lyric poetry, in the end, expresses the emotions of the "poet as self" and does so in the poet's own way. A poem is written by a specific individual. When he sees, feels, and thinks about something in a certain situation, he uses poetry to bare his soul and communicate his inner message. Whether consciously or unconsciously, he wants others to see what he sees, feel what he feels, and think what he thinks. Therefore, the art of lyric poetry strongly demands the display of the poet's individuality.

Dai Fugu said: "It must come from one's own chest; avoid following in the footsteps of others."

He Shaoji said: "A poem is your own creation, so you must speak your own words; any public or common clichés have nothing to do with you."

Gorky said: "Whoever wants to be a writer must find themselves within themselves."

Hegel said: "As soon as man engages in expressing himself, poetry begins to appear."

Of course, a poet must have a beautiful soul, high interests, a broad mind, and far-reaching ideals. He is a drop of water in the ocean, fluctuating with its temperature and rhythm; one can discover the whole ocean in that single drop.

Li Yu is capable. He quickly moved past the childhood stage of his creation.

He writes of his own sensory perceptions, his own emotions, and the throbbing of his own soul. I do not know how Li Yu was able to cross that childhood stage so quickly. Perhaps he grasped the secrets of poetry from the works of Chinese and foreign masters; perhaps he tasted its mysteries from their theories; perhaps he gradually understood the methods of poetry through his own practice; or perhaps it was a combination of all these.

Only Li Yu can explain this, and perhaps even he cannot.

In any case, having crossed this stage, he bid farewell to the commonplace and gained the power to move hearts. Only then was it possible for him to form his own artistic style.

Li Yu is wise. A wise mind listens more closely to the words of poetry, theory, life, and nature.

## C

Indeed, Li Yu's poetry now has its own face.

Reading his poems is like listening to a person with a character like a flowing spring, talking to me in a soft, steady voice. He has his own tone in speaking and his own manner in laughing.

I believe a person's natural and social personality constrains their artistic personality. Looking at his poems—especially those distinct works—my overall aesthetic impression is: Natural, Elegant, and Graceful.

**Natural**: By "natural," I mean two things. First, the emotions arise naturally. Second, they are written naturally. He writes according to the form, rhythm, and path of the emotion itself, without decoration or contortion—like clouds spreading in the sky or water flowing on the ground. The life of the natural is truth—the truth of the emotions, feelings, and thoughts of the lyrical protagonist in a specific situation.

**Elegant** (Junxiu): I mean that while his good poems do not wear special makeup, they are clear-featured, spirited, and possessed of a certain charm. For instance, in "Gleaming with Moonlight," the description of an irrigation canal flowing through the oasis under the moon gives the reader a sense of refined elegance.

**Graceful** (Wanqu): This also has two layers. One is the subtlety in expressing intent, and the other is the winding nature of the poetic inner meaning and its flow. Many of Li Yu's poems are graceful. The pulse of thought in his poetry is not like a straight man-made canal; rather, it is like a natural stream, with several winding turns.

In the use of language, Li Yu seems to have absorbed something from Uyghur folk songs. In the past, many poets drew nourishment from folk creations of enduring charm. Can contemporary poetry not do the same? It can. This is conducive to making poetry more popular and more national. Li Yu's absorption of elements from Uyghur folk songs seems harmonious with the overall tone of his poetry.

If we speak of the texture of Li Yu's poetic emotions, it is robust. What is active in his poems is the ideal, courage, and tenacity of pioneering; some poems also feature robust artistic conceptions and images. Yet the overall aesthetic feeling he gives is one of being natural, elegant, and graceful. By imbuing his poetry with these characteristics, Li Yu has secured his place in the gallery of Xinjiang's many poets.

## D

I want to say: Li Yu's poetry has largely formed its own artistic characteristics, but he also seems to have other pursuits, leaning toward aspects that do not harmonize with those characteristics. This may not be necessary. At least, not right now. It is not easy to make one's poetry possess its own artistic character and style. Why not stabilize and consolidate it for a period?

I want to say: some poems still lack richness and depth in their connotation; the reflections on life appear somewhat superficial. Our life is not a realm without differences, and a poet need not turn a blind eye to them.

I want to say: every word should be made perfectly fitting. Art, after all, requires careful carving. For example, the repetition of images is something to watch out for.

I want to say: artistic techniques could be more diverse.

## E

In Thackeray's *Vanity Fair*, there is a passage: "When a man is moved by friendship to do something, the timid become brave, the shy gain confidence, and the lazy are willing to move."

It was Li Yu's sincere friendship that moved me from trepidation to courage.

When having children, one should practice birth control—one child per couple.

But when writing poetry, the more the better! Li Yu's debut collection has been born; I look forward to his second, his third... He is in his prime—a great time!

**May 6, 1984, Shenyang-Liaoning Cooperation**
